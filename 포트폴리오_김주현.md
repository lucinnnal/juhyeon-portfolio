> First, artist-fan communication is a fiction. Fans who subscribe to fan platforms are fully aware that the artist is sending the same message to many fans, not just their own. However, to hide this fact, the platform provides an interface that makes it seem as if the artist and fan are exchanging private messages one-on-one. The virtual intimacy created by this process is what motivates fans to continue using fan platforms (Stitch, 2021). However, the non-private, short and non-serious dialogues do not contribute much to the exchange of meaning or the deepening of knowledge between the participants. On fan platforms, idols become 'accessible but unreachable', and fans use their imagination to fill the gap of not being able to reach the real thing and want to be the protagonists of private experiences related to the object. Fans may project the image of themselves that they want to be seen by the artist into the communication or feel a sense of romance in communicating with the artist (Kang 2022). In the end, the way communication works on fan platforms satisfies the possibility of reality to some extent, but it's a fiction that doesn't exist.
>
> Second, these fan platforms shift the focus of fandom consumption from content to communication, making fandom more passive. Because fan communities are groups of people who share a favorite object, they have given their members a sense of belonging even if they do not know each other (Jenkins 1992). Through participation in various media, events and fan activities, they have developed a sense of community, and based on this sense of belonging and bonding, they have shown organizational power to make their voices heard in the media and in society. However, the emergence of fan platforms has also made it difficult to distinguish between fans and non-fans, unlike in the past when a person became a fan of an artist and joined a fan community. There are few requirements to be a fan on a fan platform...

import numpy as np

theta = 0.03
n = 0.02
